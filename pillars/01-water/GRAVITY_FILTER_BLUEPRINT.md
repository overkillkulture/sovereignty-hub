### WHAT THIS BUILDS

A gravity-fed water filtration system made from two stacked food-grade 5-gallon buckets with a ceramic filter candle between them. You pour dirty water into the top bucket. Gravity pulls it through the ceramic candle (0.2 micron pores). Clean water collects in the bottom bucket and flows out through a spigot.

**Performance:**
- Flow rate: 0.5 - 1.0 gallons per hour (per candle)
- Removes: 99.99% of bacteria, 99.9% of protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium), sediment, turbidity
- Does NOT remove: viruses (add bleach treatment for virus-suspect water), dissolved chemicals, heavy metals
- Filter life: 5,000 - 10,000 gallons per candle (1-3 years typical household use)

**This filter turns creek, river, pond, rain, or questionable tap water into safe drinking water using nothing but gravity.**

- Do not use a hole saw instead of a spade bit. Hole saws grab HDPE plastic and crack the bucket. Spade bits cut cleanly.

### BUILD

#### Phase 1: Prepare the Buckets
*Estimated time: 15 minutes*

**Step 1. Inspect both buckets for cracks or contamination**
Look at the inside of both buckets. They must be clean, with no cracks, no chemical residue, no strong odors. If the bucket held food (frosting, pickles, icing), wash it with soap and warm water. If it smells like chemicals, discard it and get another one.

> CHECK: Both buckets are clean, crack-free, and odor-free. If either fails, do not proceed — get a replacement.

**Step 2. Identify which bucket is TOP (dirty water) and which is BOTTOM (clean water)**
The top bucket gets a hole in its BOTTOM (for the ceramic candle). The bottom bucket gets a hole near its BOTTOM SIDE (for the spigot). Mark them with a marker: "TOP" on one, "BOTTOM" on the other.

> MISTAKE: The #1 beginner error is drilling the wrong hole in the wrong bucket. The candle hole goes in the BOTTOM of the TOP bucket. The spigot hole goes in the SIDE of the BOTTOM bucket, near the base. Pause and confirm before drilling.

#### Phase 2: Drill the Candle Hole (Top Bucket)
*Estimated time: 10 minutes*

**Step 3. Mark the center of the TOP bucket's bottom**
Turn the TOP bucket upside down. Find the center of the flat bottom. Mark it with a marker. This is where the ceramic candle will pass through.

**Step 4. Drill the candle hole**
With the bucket upside down on a stable surface, position the 1/2" spade bit on your center mark. Drill slowly and steadily. Let the bit do the work — do not push hard or the plastic will crack. Drill straight through.

> PHOTO: The underside of the top bucket with a clean 1/2" hole in the center. No cracks or rough edges.

**Step 5. Clean the hole**
Remove any plastic burrs or rough edges from the hole using a round file, sandpaper, or a utility knife. The washer and nut need to seat flat against smooth plastic.

> CHECK: The hole is clean and round. Insert the ceramic candle stem through the hole to test fit. It should pass through snugly. If the hole is too small, carefully enlarge it with the round file. If it's too large, you'll need to rely on the washer and silicone to seal.

#### Phase 3: Install the Ceramic Candle
*Estimated time: 10 minutes*

**Step 6. Apply silicone around the candle mounting hole (inside of top bucket)**
Turn the TOP bucket right-side up. Apply a thin bead of food-grade silicone around the hole on the INSIDE bottom of the bucket. This creates a waterproof seal.

**Step 7. Insert the ceramic candle from inside the top bucket**
Place the rubber washer onto the threaded stem of the ceramic candle. Insert the candle stem down through the hole from INSIDE the bucket, so the ceramic candle body sits inside the top bucket and the threaded stem pokes out through the bottom.

> PHOTO: Looking inside the top bucket — the ceramic candle sits upright with its stem poking through the bottom hole. Washer visible between candle base and bucket floor.

**Step 8. Secure with wing nut on the outside**
Turn the top bucket upside down (carefully — hold the candle so it doesn't fall). Place a second rubber washer on the protruding threaded stem, then thread on the wing nut. Tighten by hand until snug, then ONE QUARTER TURN MORE with fingers only.

> MISTAKE: Do NOT use pliers or a wrench on the wing nut. Over-tightening will crack the ceramic candle base. Hand-tight plus a quarter turn is correct. The silicone handles the rest of the seal.

**Step 9. Apply silicone around the outside seal**
With the bucket still upside down, apply a thin bead of silicone around the nut and washer on the outside, covering the junction where the stem exits the bucket. Smooth it with a wet finger. This is your backup waterproof seal.

**Step 10. Let the silicone cure**
Set the top bucket aside (upside down) for the time specified on the silicone tube. Most food-grade silicone cures in 24 hours. Minimum wait: 2 hours for initial set before testing.

> CHECK: The candle is firmly mounted. It does not wobble. The wing nut is hand-tight plus a quarter turn. Silicone covers the outer seal completely. If the candle moves or wobbles, remove and reapply with more silicone.

#### Phase 4: Install the Spigot (Bottom Bucket)
*Estimated time: 10 minutes*

**Step 11. Mark the spigot hole position on the BOTTOM bucket**
The spigot goes on the SIDE of the bottom bucket, as LOW as possible. Measure 1.5 inches up from the base of the bucket (you need clearance so the spigot doesn't hit the surface the bucket sits on). Mark the center point for the hole.

**Step 12. Drill the spigot hole**
Using the 3/4" spade bit, drill through the side of the bottom bucket at your mark. Drill slowly. Support the inside of the bucket with your hand (wearing a glove) or a block of wood to prevent the plastic from flexing.

> PHOTO: The bottom bucket with a clean 3/4" hole in the side, approximately 1.5 inches from the base.

**Step 13. Install the spigot**
Apply a thin ring of silicone around the spigot threads. Push or thread the spigot through the hole from the OUTSIDE. On the inside, place a rubber washer, then thread on the retaining nut. Tighten by hand until snug — the silicone and washer create the seal.

**Step 14. Confirm the spigot operates**
Turn the spigot handle open and closed several times. It should turn freely. The open position should be obvious (handle parallel to flow direction = open, perpendicular = closed).

> CHECK: Spigot is mounted securely, does not leak when hand-tightened, and opens/closes smoothly. If it leaks, remove, add more silicone, reinstall, and wait for cure.

#### Phase 5: Assemble and Prime
*Estimated time: 15 minutes + 24 hours cure wait*

**Step 15. Stack the buckets**
Place the BOTTOM bucket (with spigot) on a stable, elevated surface — a table, a shelf, a stack of cinder blocks. It needs to be high enough that a glass or jar fits under the spigot.

Place the TOP bucket (with ceramic candle) on top of the bottom bucket. The top bucket should nest into the bottom bucket, with the ceramic candle hanging down inside the bottom bucket.

> PHOTO: Both buckets stacked. Top bucket nests into bottom bucket. Ceramic candle visible hanging inside the lower bucket. Spigot visible on the side of the bottom bucket.

**Step 16. Prime the ceramic candle**
Before first use, the ceramic candle must be primed. Fill the top bucket with clean tap water (NOT dirty water for the first run). Let it filter through completely. This first batch washes out ceramic dust and manufacturing residue. **Discard this first batch of filtered water.** Do not drink it.

**Step 17. Test with clean water**
Fill the top bucket again with clean tap water. Let it filter through. Open the spigot and fill a glass. The water should be clear, taste clean, and have no ceramic grit or residue.

> CHECK: Filtered water is clear, odor-free, and tastes clean. No drips from the candle mounting, the spigot, or anywhere else. If you see drips from the candle mount, the seal failed — remove, clean, reapply silicone, wait 24 hours, and test again.

**Step 18. Test with dirty water**
Now the real test. Collect water from the dirtiest source available — a creek, a puddle, a pond. Pour it into the top bucket. Wait for it to filter through. Open the spigot. The water coming out should be crystal clear.

> PHOTO: Side-by-side: a glass of the dirty source water next to a glass of the filtered water. The contrast should be dramatic.

> **This is the moment.** If the filtered water is clear and the source water was brown — your filter works. You just turned creek water into drinking water for $30.

### TESTING AND VERIFICATION

**Visual test:** Filtered water should be completely clear with zero visible sediment, regardless of how dirty the source water was.

**TDS meter test (optional, $8 on Amazon):**
- Test source water TDS reading: record ____
- Test filtered water TDS reading: record ____
- Expected: 50-80% reduction in TDS for ceramic candle alone
- Note: TDS measures dissolved solids. Ceramic filters remove particulates and bacteria but not all dissolved minerals. A moderate TDS reading in filtered water is NORMAL and not a failure.

**Taste test:** Filtered water should taste clean and neutral. No earthy, metallic, or chemical taste. If the water tastes like ceramic dust, run two more batches of clean water through — the candle needs more priming.

**Flow rate test:** A properly primed candle should produce 0.5 - 1.0 gallons per hour. If flow is significantly slower, the candle may need cleaning (see Maintenance).

**What does FAILURE look like:**
- Cloudy filtered water = candle is cracked or improperly sealed. Replace candle.
- Dripping from candle mount = silicone seal failed. Remove, clean, reseal.
- No flow at all = candle is clogged (new candles sometimes need soaking) or air-locked. Remove candle, submerge in clean water for 30 minutes, reinstall.
- Water leaking from spigot threads = tighten or reseal with silicone.

| Code | Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|------|---------|-------------|-----|
| T1 | Filtered water is cloudy | Cracked candle or bad seal | Inspect candle for hairline cracks. Check wing nut tightness. Replace if cracked. |
| T2 | No water flows through | New candle needs priming / air lock | Remove candle, submerge in clean water 30 min, reinstall. Pour water very slowly at first. |
| T3 | Very slow flow (under 0.25 gal/hr) | Candle pores are clogged with sediment | Remove candle, scrub outer surface gently with a green Scotch-Brite pad under running water until white ceramic is visible. Reinstall. |

| T5 | Leak at spigot | Loose nut or no washer | Tighten inside nut. Ensure rubber washer is in place. Add silicone if persistent. |
| T6 | Filtered water tastes like clay/ceramic | Candle not fully primed | Run 2-3 more batches of clean water through. Discard first batches. |
| T7 | Ceramic candle cracked during install | Over-tightened wing nut | Replace candle ($22-30). Hand-tight plus quarter turn ONLY. Never use tools on the nut. |
| T8 | Bucket cracked during drilling | Drilling too fast or using hole saw | Replace bucket (free from restaurant or $5). Use spade bit. Drill slowly. Support the plastic. |

### MAINTENANCE

**Monthly:**
- Inspect candle for cracks (remove top bucket, look at candle surface)
- Check spigot for drips — tighten if needed
- Check silicone seals — no peeling or gaps

**When flow slows (every 1-6 months depending on source water quality):**
- Remove ceramic candle
- Scrub outer surface gently with a green Scotch-Brite pad under running water
- Scrub until the white ceramic is visible (you're removing the layer of captured sediment)
- Do NOT use soap or detergent on the candle — just water and the pad
- Reinstall and test

**When to replace the candle:**
- After 5,000 - 10,000 gallons (1-3 years typical use)
- When the candle has been scrubbed so many times it's noticeably thinner
- If flow rate doesn't recover after scrubbing
- If ANY crack appears — replace immediately, no exceptions

**Winter storage (cold climates like North Idaho):**
- If the filter will freeze, REMOVE the ceramic candle and store it indoors
- Water expands when it freezes — a candle full of water that freezes will crack
- Drain both buckets completely
- Store candle in a clean plastic bag in a dry indoor location
- Reassemble in spring, prime with one batch of clean water before use

**Stockpile these:**
- 2-3 extra ceramic candles ($22-30 each) — these are the consumable
- Extra rubber washers
- Tube of food-grade silicone
- A green Scotch-Brite pad

### UPGRADES

**Upgrade 1: Add activated carbon for chemical removal ($10-15 more)**
The basic ceramic candle removes bacteria and sediment but not dissolved chemicals. Many ceramic candles (like the Doulton Supersterasyl) have an activated carbon core already built in. If yours doesn't, you can add a separate carbon filter stage by placing a second bucket with a carbon block filter between the ceramic bucket and the spigot bucket. This creates a 3-bucket system that also removes chlorine, pesticides, herbicides, and improves taste.

**Upgrade 2: Add a second candle for faster flow ($22-30 more)**
Drill a second hole in the top bucket, install a second ceramic candle. Doubles your flow rate to 1-2 gallons per hour. The two candles filter into the same bottom bucket.

**Upgrade 3: Add a pre-filter sock for very silty water ($5)**
For extremely muddy source water, stretch a paint strainer bag (5-gallon size, $3 for a 2-pack) over the top of the top bucket. This catches the heavy sediment before it reaches the ceramic candle, dramatically extending candle life.

**Upgrade 4: Elevate on a stand ($0-20)**
Build a simple stand from scrap wood or cinder blocks that raises the system 24-36 inches. This increases water pressure (gravity head) and improves flow rate slightly. More importantly, it makes filling glasses and bottles easier.

### APPENDIX B: TDS Meter Quick Guide

A TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) meter costs $8 on Amazon and tells you what's in your water.

**How to use:**
1. Turn it on
2. Dip the probes into the water
3. Read the number on the screen (measured in ppm — parts per million)

**What the numbers mean:**

| TDS Reading | Water Quality |
|------------|---------------|
| 0-50 ppm | Excellent (reverse osmosis level) |
| 50-170 ppm | Good (most bottled water) |
| 170-300 ppm | Acceptable (typical tap water) |
| 300-500 ppm | Fair (hard water, may taste mineral-y) |
| 500-1000 ppm | Poor (not recommended for drinking long-term) |
| 1000+ ppm | Unsafe (do not drink without advanced treatment) |

**For this filter:** Test before and after filtering. You should see a 30-80% reduction in TDS. The ceramic candle removes particulates and bacteria (which contribute to TDS) but not dissolved minerals. A TDS of 100-200 after filtering creek water is a GOOD result, not a failure.
