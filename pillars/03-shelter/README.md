- **Bonner County has had NO building codes since 1997.** No structural inspections. You need a Building Location Permit (zoning/setbacks only), not a structural permit. This is the most permissive building jurisdiction in the region.
- **Kootenai County follows IRC 2012** — stricter, permits required over 200 sq ft, snow load engineering required (60-100 psf depending on elevation)
- **Structures under 200 sq ft** often exempt from permits even in stricter counties
- **Winter reality: -10F possible, heavy snow load, 5+ months of cold** — shelter without heat is not shelter
- **Electrical** requires state-level permit regardless of county (Idaho Division of Building Safety)
- **Septic** requires Health District approval everywhere
- **Free materials everywhere:** Forest deadfall (firewood), scrap lumber from construction sites, pallets from businesses
- **Key insight: SHELTER = SHELTER + HEAT in North Idaho.** They are inseparable. A structure without heat is a freezer.

## LAYER 0: ZERO PREP — YOU HAVE NOTHING
### *Grid is down. It's 10F outside. How do you survive tonight?*

### SCENARIO A: YOU'RE IN YOUR HOUSE (Most likely)
**Your house still exists. It just has no power and no heat.**

**THE ONE-ROOM STRATEGY (Do this in the first hour):**
1. **Pick ONE room** — smallest bedroom or bathroom. Interior room with fewest windows is best.
2. **Seal it:** Hang blankets over doorways. Tape plastic sheeting or garbage bags over windows (duct tape works). Stuff towels under doors. You're creating a thermal envelope.
3. **Get bodies in there.** Each human body generates ~100 watts of heat. A family of 4 in a sealed 10x10 room raises the temp 10-15F above outside just from body heat.
4. **Layer up:** Put on EVERYTHING. The person who survives cold isn't the strongest — they're the one wearing the most layers. Wool and down insulate wet. Cotton kills.
5. **Sleeping:** Sleep together. Put cardboard, rugs, or foam under you (ground steals heat faster than air). Sleeping bags inside blankets inside more blankets.

**EMERGENCY HEAT OPTIONS (What you probably already have):**

| Heat Source | Where to Find It | BTUs | Runtime | DANGER |
|-----------|-----------------|------|---------|--------|
| **Candles** (10 tea lights in a clay pot) | Kitchen drawer | ~400 BTU total | 4-5 hours | Fire risk. Never sleep with candles burning. |
| **Gas stove/oven** | Your kitchen (if gas) | 7,000-18,000 BTU per burner | Hours on a tank | CO poisoning — crack a window 1 inch. NEVER use oven for heat overnight. |
| **Gas fireplace** | Living room (if equipped) | 20,000-40,000 BTU | Hours on existing gas | Most have battery backup igniters. Check yours NOW. |
| **Car** | Garage/driveway | Run 15 min/hour, heat car cabin | 1 gallon/hour at idle | NEVER run car in enclosed garage. CO kills in minutes. Pull car outside or crack garage door fully. |
| **Propane camp stove** | Garage/camping gear | 10,000 BTU | Hours on 1lb canister | Use in ventilated area only. |

**CRITICAL CO WARNING:** More people die from carbon monoxide poisoning during winter power outages than from cold. ANY combustion heat source needs ventilation. Crack a window. A $20 battery CO detector is the most important safety device in a power outage.

### SCENARIO B: YOU'RE OUTSIDE WITH NOTHING

**Immediate shelter options (no tools needed):**

**Debris hut** (2-3 hours, $0):
- Find a ridgepole (fallen tree or long branch, 9-12 ft)
- Lean one end on a stump or rock, other end on ground
- Lean sticks against both sides at 45 degrees
- Pile leaves, needles, boughs 3+ feet thick over the frame
- Stuff the inside with dry leaves as insulation (you're sleeping IN the leaf pile)
- Entrance faces away from wind
- **Survivable to -10F** if built thick enough. Your body heat warms the tiny air space.

**Snow cave** (if snow depth > 4 ft):
- Dig into a snowbank or drift. T-shape: entrance tunnel goes in 3 ft, sleeping platform goes up 1 ft (cold air sinks below you).
- Poke a ventilation hole in the ceiling with a stick (CO2 from breathing)
- Pack walls smooth (prevents dripping)
- Block entrance partially with backpack or snow block
- **Snow is an insulator.** Inside temp stabilizes around 32F regardless of outside temp. At -10F outside, 32F feels like a sauna.

**Car as shelter** (most realistic for most people):
- Your car is already insulated, windproof, and off the ground
- Run engine 15 minutes per hour for heat. **MUST be outdoors, not in a garage.**
- Crack a window 1 inch even while running (CO prevention)
- 1 gallon of gas = ~10 hours of intermittent heating
- Cover windows with blankets, towels, or cardboard from inside for insulation
- A single candle in a car raises interior temp ~10F

### SCENARIO C: YOUR HOUSE IS DAMAGED/DESTROYED

**Tarp shelter (10 minutes, needs 1 tarp + rope):**
- A-frame: Tie rope between two trees at chest height. Drape tarp over. Anchor edges with rocks or stakes.
- Lean-to: Tie rope at head height, drape tarp at 45 degrees to ground. Build reflector fire in front — the tarp bounces heat back.
- **A 10x12 tarp + 50 ft paracord should be in every vehicle, every go-bag.** Cost: $15-30 total.

**Shed/garage/barn conversion (first week):**
- Any structure with a roof and walls can be made livable
- Seal gaps with plastic sheeting, tarps, or even cardboard
- Insulate with whatever is available: hay bales, old clothes, newspaper, carpet scraps
- A single Mr. Buddy propane heater ($100, 9,000 BTU) makes a shed survivable
- A 20 lb propane tank ($15-30 to fill) runs a Mr. Buddy for ~100 hours on low

## LAYER 6: EUREKA INNOVATIONS — FORTUNE 500 PITCH

### EUREKA 1: CNC-Cut Flatpack Buildings — WikiHouse for Everyone
**What:** Open-source building designs (WikiHouse, wikihouse.cc) cut from plywood sheets by CNC router. Panels snap together like LEGO. No specialized construction skills needed. A team of 4 amateurs erects walls in a day. Designs are free on GitHub.
**The twist:** Cut panels in a heated shop during North Idaho winter. Stockpile them. When spring hits, assemble in days. Or: cut panels for OTHER people's builds and sell them. A CNC router ($5,000-8,000) + plywood ($3,000-5,000 per house) = you're a housing factory. The CNC cuts 24/7 in a garage while you sleep.
**Why Fortune 500:** $1.8T global construction market. Housing shortage is a global crisis. Open-source + CNC = decentralized manufacturing of homes.

### EUREKA 2: Rocket Mass Heaters — 80% Less Firewood
**What:** A rocket mass heater burns wood at 1,400F+ in an insulated combustion chamber, routes exhaust through a thermal mass (cob bench, stone, concrete), and radiates heat for 12+ hours after the fire goes out. Uses 1/4 to 1/5 the wood of a conventional stove. A 2-hour burn heats a space for 24 hours.
**The twist:** The Liberator (rocketheater.com) is the only UL-listed rocket heater — meaning it's insurance-approvable and code-compliant. It burns pellets without electricity. For North Idaho: 1 cord of wood does the work of 4-5 cords. Your firewood supply lasts 4-5x longer. In a crisis, that's the difference between surviving one month and surviving all winter.
**Why Fortune 500:** Wood heating market is $4.7B. 12.5 million US homes use wood as primary or secondary heat. Efficiency gains of 80% on the most common emergency fuel source.

### EUREKA 3: Earthship Homes — Zero Energy Bills Forever
**What:** Tire walls rammed with earth (600+ tires per house), earth-bermed on 3 sides, passive solar on the south face. Interior stays 60-70F year-round through thermal mass — even in North Idaho winters. Rainwater collection, greywater recycling, and food production built into the structure.
**The twist:** Bonner County has no structural building codes. You CAN build an earthship here. The tires are free (tire shops pay to dispose of them). The earth is free. The main cost is labor and the south-facing glazing. A 1,000 sq ft earthship: $50,000-100,000 DIY (mostly glazing, plumbing, electrical). Compare to a conventional home at $200,000-400,000.
**Why Fortune 500:** Net-zero housing is a $100B+ market. Earthships are the most proven passive design — 50+ years of data from Taos, NM. Adapting to cold climates (Idaho, Montana, Colorado) opens massive new territory.

### EUREKA 4: 3D Printed Concrete Structures
**What:** Companies like ICON, Apis Cor, and Alquist 3D print concrete walls in 24-72 hours. A 600 sq ft structure: ~$10,000-15,000 in materials. Walls are stronger than conventional (continuous pour, no joints). Complex curves and shapes cost the same as straight walls.
**The twist:** You don't print the whole house — you print the walls (the hardest, most expensive part) and conventional-frame the roof, windows, and interior. Walls in 48 hours. Finished in 4-6 weeks. North Idaho limitation: can't print below 40F, so summer-only for wall printing. But the walls last forever — concrete doesn't rot, burn, or get eaten by insects.
**Why Fortune 500:** $15B 3D printing construction market by 2030. Housing shortage + labor shortage + material costs all point to automated construction.

### EUREKA 5: AI-Generated Building Plans — Describe It, Build It
**What:** Describe your lot: "40x60 flat area, south-facing slope behind it, 2 big pines on the east side, creek 100 yards north, family of 4, cold winters." AI generates: structural plans, material list with quantities and costs, CNC cut files for every panel, foundation specifications for your soil type, solar positioning for passive heating, plumbing and electrical layouts. What takes an architect 2-4 weeks: AI does in minutes.
**The twist:** The AI designs for YOUR specific lot, YOUR climate, YOUR budget. It knows North Idaho snow loads (60-100 psf). It knows Bonner County has no structural code but you still want it to not collapse. It optimizes for passive solar at your exact latitude (48.18°N). It knows where to put windows so you get solar gain in winter but shade in summer. A custom home design for the cost of a conversation.
**Why Fortune 500:** Architectural design market is $40B. AI reduces the cost from $5,000-50,000 per design to nearly zero. Every person building anything becomes a customer.
